FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the questions families ask us most.

What is ABA therapy and how does it work?

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) is a therapy based on the science of learning and behavior. ABA therapy improves specific behaviors such as communication, social skills, reading, academics, and daily living skills. It uses positive reinforcement — when a behavior is followed by something valued (a reward), the behavior is more likely to be repeated.

How many hours of ABA therapy does my child need?

The recommended number of hours varies based on your child's individual needs, age, and goals. Research suggests that intensive ABA (25–40 hours per week) produces the best outcomes for young children with autism. However, many children benefit from part-time programs (10–20 hours per week). Your child's BCBA will recommend the appropriate intensity after a comprehensive assessment.

Is ABA therapy covered by insurance in New Jersey?

Yes! New Jersey law requires most insurance plans to cover ABA therapy for individuals with autism spectrum disorder. This includes Medicaid (NJ FamilyCare), as well as most commercial insurance plans. Our team will verify your benefits at no cost to you before services begin.

How long does ABA therapy take to show results?

Every child progresses at their own pace. Some families notice improvements within weeks; others see significant changes over months. ABA is a long-term investment — research shows the best outcomes come from early, intensive intervention. Your BCBA will track progress with data and adjust the program regularly to maximize results.

What is the difference between a BCBA and an RBT?

A Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA) is a master's-level clinician who designs and oversees your child's treatment plan. A Registered Behavior Technician (RBT) is a trained paraprofessional who delivers the therapy directly under the supervision of the BCBA. At Journeys ABA, every program is designed and supervised by a BCBA.

Can ABA therapy help with behaviors like tantrums and aggression?

Yes. ABA is highly effective at reducing challenging behaviors by identifying their root causes (the "function" of the behavior) and teaching replacement behaviors. Rather than simply punishing unwanted behavior, ABA helps children learn more effective ways to communicate their needs.

How do I know if my child needs ABA therapy?

If your child has been diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder, a developmental delay, or is showing challenges with communication, social skills, or daily living skills, ABA therapy may be beneficial. Contact us for a free consultation — our team can help you determine if ABA is the right fit.

Will I be involved in my child's ABA therapy?

Absolutely — and we strongly encourage it. Parent and caregiver involvement is a core part of our approach. We provide regular training so you can reinforce skills at home, and we hold monthly meetings to review progress and update goals together.

Understanding Your Child's ABA Assessment

The assessment is the very first step of your child's ABA journey — and nothing about it is a test your child can fail. A Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA) spends time getting to know your child: how they communicate, what they love, what frustrates them, and which skills are ready to grow.

Expect a mix of play-based observation, structured activities, and a long conversation with you. Parents are the experts on their own child, so your answers shape the whole picture. The BCBA may also review records from your pediatrician, school, or early intervention program.

Afterward, the BCBA writes a treatment plan: clear, measurable goals across communication, daily living, social skills, and behavior. You'll review it together, and nothing is finalized without your input. The assessment results also go to your insurance to authorize therapy hours.

How to prepare: jot down your top three concerns and your top three hopes. Bring examples of what a hard day looks like — and what a great day looks like. That contrast tells us more than any checklist.

How to Talk to Your Child About Therapy

Children take their cues from you: if therapy sounds like something exciting and positive, that's how they'll walk in. Keep it simple — “You're going to play and learn with someone who helps kids grow stronger at the things that feel tricky.”

Avoid framing therapy as something that happens to them, or as a consequence for behavior. It is never a punishment, and it's important your child doesn't hear it described that way — even jokingly.

For younger children, a short social story with pictures of the new place and new faces works wonders. Read it a few times in the days before the first session. For older children, answer questions honestly and let them voice worries — naming a feeling is the first step to managing it.

Try this: after each session, ask “What was the most fun thing today?” instead of “How was it?” Specific questions get real answers, and they teach your child to notice the good parts.

Caregiver Self-Care: Supporting Yourself While Supporting Your Child

You can't pour from an empty cup. Parents of children with special needs report some of the highest stress levels of any group — and pretending otherwise helps no one, least of all your child, who reads your stress better than anyone.

Self-care doesn't need to be a spa day. It's ten minutes of quiet coffee before the house wakes up, a walk during a therapy session instead of errands, or saying yes when a relative offers to babysit. Small, repeatable moments beat rare grand gestures.

Build your village: a partner, a friend who gets it, a support group of parents on the same road. Other ABA parents are an extraordinary source of practical wisdom and zero judgment.

Remember: asking for help is modeling exactly the skill we teach your child every day — using your words to get your needs met. You deserve the same support you're working so hard to give.

Navigating the IEP Process in New Jersey

In New Jersey, a child who needs special-education services gets them through an Individualized Education Program (IEP). The process starts with a written referral to your district's Child Study Team — you can make this referral yourself, in writing, at any time.

Once you consent to an evaluation, the district has 90 days to evaluate and, if eligible, put an IEP in place. The IEP lists your child's present levels, measurable annual goals, services, and accommodations. You are a full member of the IEP team — not a guest.

Bring your ABA team's input: BCBA reports, progress data, and skill assessments give the school concrete, objective information. With your consent, we're glad to coordinate directly with your child's teachers so strategies stay consistent between home, school, and therapy.

Know your rights: you may request meetings, bring anyone you like to them, and disagree in writing. New Jersey's Parental Rights in Special Education (PRISE) booklet spells out every protection — your district must give you a copy.

Reinforcement Strategies You Can Use at Home

Reinforcement is the engine of ABA: behavior that gets followed by something good happens more often. You already use it every day — the trick is using it on purpose.

Catch your child being good. Attention is powerful, so aim it at the moments you want more of: “You put your plate in the sink — that was so helpful!” Specific praise beats a generic “good job” because it tells your child exactly what worked.

Reinforce immediately and consistently. A reward twenty minutes later loses its connection to the behavior. And keep reinforcers fresh: rotate small privileges, activities, and praise so they don't wear out.

One caution: be careful not to accidentally reinforce the behaviors you want less of. Giving in after whining teaches that whining works. Hold the line calmly, then reinforce generously the moment your child uses a better strategy — that contrast is where the learning lives.

Insurance & ABA: What NJ Families Need to Know

New Jersey law requires state-regulated health plans to cover medically necessary ABA therapy for children with an autism diagnosis. Coverage details vary by plan, but the right to coverage itself is well established.

The usual path: a formal autism diagnosis from a physician or licensed psychologist, then an ABA assessment, then an authorization request from your provider to the insurer. Authorizations are typically approved for six months at a time and renewed with progress reports.

Expect some paperwork — and know that we handle most of it. Journeys ABA verifies your benefits before services start, submits the authorization, and tracks renewal dates so therapy doesn't lapse.

If a claim is denied: don't panic, and don't take the first “no” as final. You have the right to an internal appeal and then an external review through New Jersey's Independent Health Care Appeals Program. Most well-documented ABA denials are overturned. Call us — we've walked many families through it.
